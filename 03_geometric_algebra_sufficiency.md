# Chapter 3: G(3) and Algebraic Closure

## 3.1 The Question

Chapter 1 proposed the four intrinsic variables {S, **A**, **I**, **R**} as a *minimal generative hypothesis*: no variable can be removed (necessity, established in §11.3.1), and no fifth variable is needed (sufficiency, deferred to this chapter).

The sufficiency claim is the harder of the two. Necessity is demonstrated by showing that removing any variable makes the UoC non-operative. Sufficiency requires showing that adding a fifth variable would be redundant — that any ontological property **captured by the configuration space defined in Ch1–Ch2** is already expressible in terms of the four.

This chapter develops an **algebraic closure argument** grounded in **geometric algebra** (GA) — also called Clifford algebra. The argument does not validate sufficiency empirically, nor does it prove the four variables are ontologically necessary by themselves — that remains a philosophical argument (Chapter 1). What the chapter establishes is this: (i) G(3) is not a representational choice but the unique Clifford algebra of the configuration space ℝ³ derived in Ch2 (§3.3.5, Lemma 3.1); (ii) within that algebra, the four variables generate the complete structure — every derived property expressible in the configuration space is already contained in the algebra generated by {S, **A**, **I**, **R**}, and no fifth generator is required.

---

## 3.2 Geometric Algebra G(3): Setup

The geometric algebra of 3-dimensional space, denoted **G(3)**, is constructed from the vector space ℝ³ by introducing a single operation: the **geometric product**.

Given two vectors **u**, **v** ∈ ℝ³, their geometric product is:

$$\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{u} \wedge \mathbf{v}$$

where:
- **u**·**v** is the symmetric part (inner/dot product) — a scalar
- **u**∧**v** is the antisymmetric part (wedge/exterior product) — a **bivector** (oriented area)

This single operation unifies scalar and vector multiplication. The resulting algebra has 8 basis elements, organized by **grade** (a generalization of dimension):

| Grade | Basis elements | Count | Geometric meaning |
|-------|----------------|-------|-------------------|
| 0 | 1 | 1 | Scalar |
| 1 | **e**₁, **e**₂, **e**₃ | 3 | Vectors (directed lines) |
| 2 | **e**₁**e**₂, **e**₁**e**₃, **e**₂**e**₃ | 3 | Bivectors (oriented areas) |
| 3 | **e**₁**e**₂**e**₃ | 1 | Pseudoscalar (oriented volume) |
| **Total** | | **8 = 2³** | |

**Proposition 3.1 (Closure).** The algebra G(3) is closed: every product of elements in G(3) produces another element in G(3). Equivalently, the generating set {**e**₁, **e**₂, **e**₃} spans G(3) under the geometric product.

## 3.3 The Four Variables as Generators of G(3)

From Chapter 2: S is a scalar (grade 0), and **A**, **I**, **R** are vectors (grade 1) in ℝ³. We now examine what these four elements generate under the geometric product.

### 3.3.1 Scalars (Grade 0)

S is directly a scalar. Additionally, the geometric products of **A**, **I**, **R** contain scalar parts:

$$\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{I}, \quad \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{R}, \quad \mathbf{I} \cdot \mathbf{R} \in \mathbb{R}$$

These are not new variables — they are derived relationships between the existing vectors.

### 3.3.2 Vectors (Grade 1)

**A**, **I**, **R** and all their linear combinations span ℝ³. If **A**, **I**, **R** are linearly independent — the non-degenerate case, formalized as det(**A**, **I**, **R**) ≠ 0 — they constitute a basis for ℝ³ and every vector in the configuration space is accessible. The degenerate cases are analyzed in §3.6.

### 3.3.3 Bivectors (Grade 2)

The wedge products of pairs of vectors produce three bivectors:

$$\mathbf{A} \wedge \mathbf{I}, \quad \mathbf{A} \wedge \mathbf{R}, \quad \mathbf{I} \wedge \mathbf{R}$$

Bivectors represent oriented areas — the "planes" spanned by pairs of vectors. They are new objects, not reducible to scalars or vectors, but they are *generated* by the four intrinsic variables. Under the linear independence assumption (det(**A**,**I**,**R**) ≠ 0), these three bivectors span the 3D grade-2 subspace — they form a basis for that subspace, though not the standard orthonormal basis unless **A**,**I**,**R** are themselves orthonormal (see §3.3.5).

### 3.3.4 The Pseudoscalar (Grade 3)

The wedge product of all three vectors:

$$\mathbf{A} \wedge \mathbf{I} \wedge \mathbf{R} = |\mathbf{A} \wedge \mathbf{I} \wedge \mathbf{R}| \cdot \mathbf{e}_1\mathbf{e}_2\mathbf{e}_3$$

This is a scalar multiple of the unit pseudoscalar **i** = **e**₁**e**₂**e**₃. It encodes the oriented volume spanned by the three vectors — a single scalar quantity representing how fully the UoC "inhabits" its configuration space.

### 3.3.5 Generation of the Complete Algebra

**Lemma 3.1 (G(3) is determined by Ch2, not chosen).** Ch2 §2.3.2 established that the configuration space of **A**, **I**, **R** is ℝ³, equipped with the Euclidean metric and a fixed orientation (Ch2 §2.6). Given a real vector space V with a positive-definite metric g, the Clifford algebra Cl(V, g) is uniquely determined. For V = ℝ³ and g = g_Eucl, that algebra is G(3) = Cl(3,0). Therefore G(3) is not a representational choice — it is the unique Clifford algebra of the configuration space derived in Ch2. The representational hypothesis of Proposition 3.2 reduces to the representational hypothesis of Ch2: that the UoC's configuration space is ℝ³.

Starting from {S, **A**, **I**, **R**} with **A**, **I**, **R** linearly independent (det(**A**, **I**, **R**) ≠ 0), the geometric product generates representatives of all 8 grade dimensions of G(3):

$$\{1,\; \mathbf{A},\; \mathbf{I},\; \mathbf{R},\; \mathbf{A}\wedge\mathbf{I},\; \mathbf{A}\wedge\mathbf{R},\; \mathbf{I}\wedge\mathbf{R},\; \mathbf{A}\wedge\mathbf{I}\wedge\mathbf{R}\}$$

These are not the standard orthonormal basis elements {1, **e**₁, **e**₂, **e**₃, **e**₁**e**₂, **e**₁**e**₃, **e**₂**e**₃, **e**₁**e**₂**e**₃} of G(3) unless **A**, **I**, **R** are themselves orthonormal; they are a basis for each grade subspace under the linear independence assumption.

**Proposition 3.2 (Formal Minimal Sufficiency).** *Given* det(**A**, **I**, **R**) ≠ 0: the set {**A**, **I**, **R**} generates G(3) = Cl(ℝ³) as a Clifford algebra, and S is a grade-0 element already present in the algebra as a scalar multiple of the identity. No additional grade-1 generator is required to span any grade of G(3): the grade-k subspaces are spanned by the k-fold wedge products of **A**, **I**, **R** for k = 1, 2, 3.

*What remains open*: G(3) being the algebra of the configuration space is now derived (Lemma 3.1). What is not derived is whether the configuration space ℝ³ itself captures all ontologically relevant structure of a UoC. That remains the open empirical question — not "is G(3) the right algebra?" but "is ℝ³ the right configuration space?" (§3.5).

*Proof sketch.* **A**, **I**, **R** ∈ G(3) as grade-1 elements forming a basis for ℝ³ (by the linear independence assumption). Their pairwise geometric products generate the grade-2 subspace: ⟨**A****I**⟩₂ = **A**∧**I**, and similarly for the other pairs — three linearly independent bivectors spanning the 3D grade-2 subspace. The grade-3 element **A**∧**I**∧**R** = ⟨**A****I****R**⟩₃ is nonzero (det(**A**,**I**,**R**) ≠ 0), spanning the 1D grade-3 subspace. The scalar parts ⟨**u****v**⟩₀ = **u**·**v** together with S span the grade-0 subspace. This covers all 8 grade dimensions of G(3). ∎

### 3.3.6 The State as a Paravector — M = S + V

The state vector ξ = (S, **A**, **I**, **R**) admits a natural embedding in G(3) as a **paravector** — a multivector with only grade-0 and grade-1 parts:

$$M = S + \mathbf{V}, \qquad \mathbf{V} = \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{I} + \mathbf{R}$$

where S is the scalar part (grade 0) and **V** is the vector part (grade 1). This is the canonical paravector form in GA, written M = α + **v** in standard notation.

*Note on notation.* M is distinct from the configuration matrix Γ(ξ) ∈ Sym(4)⊕so(4) defined in Ch1 §1.5 and developed in Ch5. M is the GA representation of the state ξ — a grade-(0+1) multivector in G(3). Γ(ξ) is a 4×4 matrix encoding pairwise coupling structure, a different mathematical object derived from ξ. The paravector M captures the grade-0 and grade-1 content of ξ; the bivector and pseudoscalar grades (2 and 3) are generated by products of M's components, as developed below.

The asymmetry between S and the other three variables, derived on structural grounds in Chapter 2, maps naturally onto the grade structure of G(3).

**Scalar part and vector part.** The geometric product of M with its vector part **V** is:

$$M\mathbf{V} = S\mathbf{V} + \mathbf{V}^2$$

**First term — SV** (singularity scales the vectorial deployment):
$$S\mathbf{V} = S\mathbf{A} + S\mathbf{I} + S\mathbf{R}$$

The component S**A** is precisely **Force**. The terms S**I** and S**R** are structurally generated by the same operation and await interpretation.

**Second term — V²** (self-product of the vector part): Since **V** = **A** + **I** + **R** is a grade-1 element (a vector), its square under the geometric product is purely scalar:

$$\mathbf{V}^2 = |\mathbf{V}|^2 = |\mathbf{A}|^2 + |\mathbf{I}|^2 + |\mathbf{R}|^2 + 2(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{I} + \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{R} + \mathbf{I}\cdot\mathbf{R})$$

This follows because for any vector **v**, **v**² = **v**·**v** = |**v**|². The cross terms **u****v** + **v****u** = 2(**u**·**v**) are symmetric (the antisymmetric bivector parts cancel in the symmetric sum).

**Bivectors from pairwise products.** The grade-2 elements do not appear in **V**² but arise from asymmetric products of pairs of vectors:

$$\mathbf{A}\mathbf{I} = \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{I} + \mathbf{A}\wedge\mathbf{I}$$

and similarly for **A****R** and **I****R**. The grade-2 part ⟨**A****I**⟩₂ = **A**∧**I** is a bivector — an oriented plane that cannot be reduced to a scalar or a vector. In particular, the bivector **I**∧**R** is the precursor to **Field** via the Hodge dual (§3.4.2).

*Structural observation*: Force (S**A**) appears directly in the product M**V**. The precursors to Field (**I**∧**R**) arise from the grade-2 part of the pairwise product **I****R**. The inherent variables emerge from the algebra of {S, **A**, **I**, **R**} under the identification of Force and Field as ontologically meaningful observables — an identification that is a structural postulate of the framework (Ch1 §1.4), not an automatic consequence of the algebra alone. The central contribution of the GA formalism is that no new generators beyond {**A**, **I**, **R**} are required to produce them.

## 3.4 Force and Field in Geometric Algebra Terms

The inherent variables introduced in Chapter 1 are specific operations within G(3).

### 3.4.1 Force as Scalar Multiplication

$$\text{Force} = S \cdot \mathbf{A}$$

This is the simplest operation in G(3): scaling a grade-1 element by a grade-0 element. The result is a grade-1 element (vector). Force does not introduce new algebraic structure — it is the scaling of the agency vector **A** by the boundary scalar S.

### 3.4.2 Field as Hodge Dual of the Wedge Product

The cross product **I**×**R** used to define Field is, in GA terms, the **Hodge dual** of the wedge product **I**∧**R**:

$$\text{Field} = \mathbf{I} \times \mathbf{R} = (\mathbf{I} \wedge \mathbf{R}) \cdot \mathbf{i}^{-1}$$

where **i** = **e**₁**e**₂**e**₃ is the unit pseudoscalar of G(3), and **i**² = −1, so **i**⁻¹ = −**i**.

Concretely:

$$\text{Field} = -(\mathbf{I} \wedge \mathbf{R}) \cdot \mathbf{i}$$

**What this means structurally**: Field is grade-2 as a bivector (**I**∧**R**) and grade-1 as its dual. The Hodge duality maps the "oriented area" spanned by **I** and **R** to a vector perpendicular to both. This perpendicular vector is what acts in the configuration space of the UoC.

*Note on the Hodge dual.* The operation (**I**∧**R**)·**i**⁻¹ presupposes the Euclidean metric and orientation of ℝ³ (as declared in Ch2 §2.6). Under these conditions, the Hodge dual maps bivectors to grade-1 vectors unambiguously.

## 3.5 The Algebraic Closure Argument

The structural justification for the minimal sufficiency hypothesis rests on the **closure of G(3)** under its generating set. Two claims must be distinguished carefully.

**Formal sufficiency.** Within the chosen GA representation — the configuration space ℝ³ with the geometric product — the three vector variables {**A**, **I**, **R**} generate G(3), and S is a grade-0 element already in the algebra. Every element of G(3) is expressible as a product of elements in {**A**, **I**, **R**} (Proposition 3.2). No fifth grade-1 generator is required by the algebraic structure of ℝ³.

*This is a conditional result, but with one condition now derived.* The sufficiency holds given: (a) the configuration space is ℝ³, derived in Ch2 from Postulate 2.1 and the cross-product constraint. Condition (b) — that the algebra is G(3) — follows from (a) by Lemma 3.1 and is no longer a free hypothesis. What remains open is whether (a) itself is complete: whether ℝ³ captures all ontologically relevant structure of the UoC, or whether a richer configuration space (ℝ⁴, ℝ⁷, or a curved manifold) is eventually required. If the configuration space must be extended, G(3) would be replaced by the Clifford algebra of that space — not by an independent representational choice.

**Ontological sufficiency.** Whether the four variables exhaust the real ontological structure of a UoC remains an empirical and philosophical question, not settled by the algebraic argument. The algebraic closure argument shows that *within the chosen configuration model*, no fifth variable adds expressive power. It does not show that the chosen model is complete with respect to the real structure of consciousness. This distinction is maintained explicitly throughout the chapter.

To state the claim precisely: if the ontological properties of interest are those **captured by the configuration space defined in Ch1–Ch2**, then every such property is an element of G(3), and {S, **A**, **I**, **R**} generate all of them. No stronger claim is made.

A fifth intrinsic variable X would require independent generation within the configuration space only if it satisfied one of two conditions:

1. **X is a new grade-1 element** (a new vector in the configuration space): this would extend the configuration space from ℝ³ to ℝ⁴ or higher, generating G(4) (16 elements) or above. This is structurally possible — it would correspond to a new "direction" of agency, impulse, or relation that cannot be expressed as a combination of the existing three. Whether such a direction exists is an empirical question left open.

2. **X is a grade-2 or grade-3 element** (a bivector or pseudoscalar): this is already generated by products of the existing variables. X would be a derived property, not an independent generator.

*Candidate fifth variables and their reduction*:

| Candidate | Claimed new variable | Reduction |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Temporality | The UoC's position in time | Parameter of ξ(t), not a state variable; epistemological (memory) when processed |
| Perception | Registration of information | Epistemological level; emerges when EUoC is non-null |
| Value/preference | What the UoC favors | Already encoded in the direction of **I** (impulse breaks symmetry in favor of a direction) |
| Affect/emotion | Felt quality of the field | Epistemological expression of Field; not independent at the ontological level |
| Power | Magnitude of Force | |**Force**| = S|**A**|; derived, not independent |
| Intention | Deliberate direction of action | Epistemological (requires EUoC); distinct from **I** (ontological impulse) |

*Note*: the reductions in this table are structural arguments — correspondences between candidate properties and elements already present in G(3) or in the epistemological/teleological levels — not formal proofs of non-existence. A candidate that genuinely escaped all four reductions and satisfied the intrinsecity criterion (Ch1 §1.2) would constitute evidence for extending the model to G(4).

The formal closure argument would require revision if an ontological property **captured by the chosen configuration model** were identified that (a) is intrinsic, (b) is not epistemological or teleological, and (c) cannot be expressed as any element of G(3) generated by {S, **A**, **I**, **R**}. Without such a property, the four variables provide a closed generative system within the formal configuration space. Whether a future property forces an extension to G(4) or a different algebra is an open empirical question (§3.8).

## 3.6 The Degenerate Case: When the Algebra Collapses

The sufficiency argument assumes **A**, **I**, **R** are linearly independent. When they are not, the algebra collapses.

---

**Proposition 3.3 (Algebraic Degeneracy).** *If* **I** *∥* **R** *and* **A** *is not parallel to* **I** *(i.e.,* **I**∧**R** *= 0 and* **A**∧**I** *≠ 0), then {*****A***,* **I***,* **R***} span* ℝ² *and generate* G(2) *(4 elements) rather than* G(3) *(8 elements). Equivalently, the structural Field vanishes and the UoC operates in a reduced configuration algebra.*

*Extreme case.* If **A** ∥ **I** ∥ **R** (all three parallel), then {**A**, **I**, **R**} span ℝ¹ and generate G(1) — 2 elements {1, **e**₁}. This represents a maximally degenerate UoC: no structural Field, no bivector structure, and Force = S**A** is the only operative inherent variable. The pseudoscalar **A**∧**I**∧**R** = 0 in both degenerate cases.

*Proof.* **I** ∥ **R** implies **I**∧**R** = 0, so Field = (**I**∧**R**)**i**⁻¹ = 0. Under **A**∧**I** ≠ 0, {**A**, **I**, **R**} span a 2D subspace — a plane containing the collinear **I** and **R** and the transverse **A**. The geometric algebra of a 2D space is G(2), with basis {1, **e**₁, **e**₂, **e**₁**e**₂} — 4 elements. The pseudoscalar **A**∧**I**∧**R** = 0. Under **A** ∥ **I** ∥ **R**, the span collapses to ℝ¹ generating G(1). ∎

---

What is described as a degenerate UoC configuration (Ch1 §1.4.1) corresponds precisely to algebraic degeneration — a reduction of the configuration algebra from G(3) to G(2). The degree of algebraic degeneration tracks the degree of structural pathology, offering a possible formal grounding for a theory of UoC structural failure modes.

**Case: Full linear independence**

When **A**, **I**, **R** span ℝ³:

$$\mathbf{A} \wedge \mathbf{I} \wedge \mathbf{R} \neq 0$$

The pseudoscalar is non-zero, and the UoC fully inhabits its configuration space. The magnitude |**A**∧**I**∧**R**| is a measure of how fully the UoC's three directional variables span the available space — a possible formal definition of *ontological coherence* within the model.

The three vector variables {**A**, **I**, **R**} generate G(3) (Proposition 3.2); S is a grade-0 element present in the algebra by construction. Every property of a UoC **captured by the G(3) model of Ch1–Ch2** is an element of this algebra — conditional on the representational hypotheses stated in §3.5. No fifth grade-1 generator is required *within the chosen model*. Whether future evidence demands an extension to G(4) or a different algebra remains an open empirical question (§3.8).

## 3.8 Open Questions

- **Empirical sufficiency**: the formal closure of G(3) does not rule out that a future observation identifies an intrinsic property not capturable within the configuration space of Ch1–Ch2 and requiring G(4) or a different algebra. The 7-dimensional extension via octonions remains an open direction (Ch2 §2.7).
- **Ontological coherence**: the pseudoscalar **A**∧**I**∧**R** as a measure of structural coherence within the model is a candidate formal definition; its development and connection to the dynamics of ξ(t) is left for future work.
- **Bivector properties**: the bivectors **A**∧**I** and **A**∧**R** have no structural interpretation yet beyond their role in generating the algebra. Their dynamical significance is open.
