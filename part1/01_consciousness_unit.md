# Chapter 1: Units of Coherence and Consciousness

## 1.1 Conceptual Framework

This framework describes how consciousness expresses itself through structure. It does not take a position on whether consciousness is fundamental or emergent, nor on the nature of its substrate. Instead, it describes the structural conditions under which consciousness organizes itself into a discrete, operative unit.

The fundamental observation motivating the framework is the following recursive pattern:

> A substrate $\mathcal{C}$ (fundamental or emergent) organizes itself under structural restrictions into a **UoC** — a unit of structural coherence or unit of consciousness depending on whether the epistemological level is null or positive (§1.2). The output of the UoC is a higher-order organizational state $\mathcal{C}'$ capable of functioning as substrate for a UoC at the next level: $\mathcal{C}_{n+1} = U(\mathcal{C}_n)$.

*Unit of consciousness reading:* when the epistemological level is positive at each level, the recursion generates emergent consciousness — the substrate $\mathcal{C}$ and emergent output $\mathcal{C}'$ are conscious organizational states. *Unit of coherence reading:* when the epistemological level is null, the recursion generates structural organization without epistemological modulation — as in physical systems and simple biological hierarchies.

This recursion is open-ended in both directions: downward toward a substrate that need not be specified, and upward toward levels that exceed the scope of this work. The term "consciousness" is used for the substrate and the output for continuity with the framework's domain of application; the formal object being defined is the structural operator $U(\cdot)$, not the substrate itself. The framework operates at the level of the UoC itself, not at the level of $\mathcal{C}$ or $\mathcal{C}'$.

**Scope.** This is a framework of how consciousness *organizes itself* structurally — not of what consciousness is. The question "what is consciousness?" belongs to the hard problem (Chalmers, 1995). The question "how does consciousness organize itself into discrete operative units?" belongs to structural analysis. These are distinct explanatory targets; the framework addresses the second without requiring an answer to the first.

**On using "consciousness" without defining it.** Classical mechanics formalizes the relations between mass, force, and acceleration without defining what mass fundamentally is. The GSF follows the same logic: it formalizes the structural relations between S, **A**, **I**, **R** without requiring a prior theory of the substrate. Requiring consciousness to be fundamental would make the framework inapplicable in a materialist context; requiring it to be emergent would make it inapplicable in an idealist context. Agnosticism on the substrate question preserves the framework's generality across both readings.

The recursion consciousness → UoC → emergent consciousness is not circular: the output of one level is the substrate of the next, and the framework describes structural conditions at each level. It does not require the chain to have a first term.

## 1.2 Definition

**Definition 1.1 (UoC — Unit of Structural Coherence / Unit of Consciousness).**
A unit of structural coherence (UoC) is a system that:

1. Takes a substrate $\mathcal{C}$ capable of supporting conscious organization — fundamental or emergent — as its input.
2. Applies structural restrictions, organizing that substrate into a discrete, self-maintaining configuration.
3. Generates as its output a higher-order organizational state $\mathcal{C}'$ capable of functioning as substrate for a subsequent UoC.

*Unit of consciousness.* A UoC with positive epistemological level additionally generates the capacity for self-knowledge and purpose orientation — it operates as a unit of consciousness. The formal structure (intrinsic variables, configuration matrix, dynamics) is identical in both cases.

A UoC is characterized by four **intrinsic variables** — properties that arise from the UoC's own organization and persist as long as that organization persists — and two **inherent variables** — functionally derived state variables whose values are determined at each moment by the values of the intrinsic variables, and are therefore always present when the UoC is operative.

**Intrinsecity criterion**: a variable is intrinsic to a UoC if and only if (i) it arises from the system's own organization, not from an external designer or imposing agent; (ii) it persists as long as the system's organization persists; and (iii) its removal would require the destruction of that organization. This criterion distinguishes a UoC from systems whose defining properties are externally imposed — a thermostat has a boundary (S), a response capacity (A), a target state (I), and environmental coupling (R), but none of these arise from the thermostat's own organization: all four are externally specified by its designer. A living cell satisfies the criterion: its membrane is self-assembled, its regulatory capacity is emergent from its metabolic network, and its homeostatic drive arises from its chemistry. The intrinsecity criterion is the formal boundary between a UoC and a system that merely models the same structure.

A UoC has structural levels. Three are identified and formalized in the present work; the framework is open to additional levels being identified in future development. The three levels developed here are:

- **Ontological level**: the intrinsic variables and the inherent variables. Always non-null.
- **Epistemological level**: the processes by which the UoC knows itself and its environment. This level is formally ≥ 0: when null, epistemological modulation is absent — $\Gamma_E = \mathrm{Id}$ — and the UoC operates as a **unit of structural coherence**; when positive, the UoC operates as a **unit of consciousness**, generating the potential to orient toward its structural purpose. The two designations refer to the same formal object at different values of this parameter, not to two distinct objects.
- **Teleological level**: the purpose of the UoC, modeled mathematically as an attractor structure in state space. Formally ≥ 0: a mathematical attractor is always present as a structural feature of the dynamics (§1.6); the teleological level is the *interpretation* of certain attractors as purpose-bearing structures accessible to the system. When null: no attractor is epistemically accessible as structural purpose. When positive: the system's dynamics are oriented toward a purpose-bearing attractor.

**On the closure of the level structure.** The three levels above are *the levels identified at the time of writing this work*. They are not claimed to be exhaustive. The framework is intentionally open to the identification of additional structural levels — for example, a *relational* level distinct from the ontological one (the constitution of inter-UoC couplings as a first-class structural object), or an *axiological* level distinct from the teleological one (the structure by which a UoC values or orders its purposes). Whether such candidates correspond to genuinely new levels or to refinements of the three already named is a question the framework leaves open. What is fixed is that the three levels presented here are sufficient to develop the formalism of Parts I–II; what is not fixed is the completeness of the list.

Whether any UoC's epistemological level is genuinely zero — as opposed to positive but undetectable — is an open empirical question. Absence of detected epistemological activity is not the same as absence of epistemological structure; treating it as zero for physical systems is a working compatibility assumption with the natural sciences, not an ontological claim.

The same holds for the teleological level. The mathematical attractor modeled in §1.6 is always present as a structural feature of the dynamics; a zero teleological level means no attractor is epistemically accessible as structural purpose — not that the attractor is absent. Again, treating the teleological level as zero for physical systems is a working assumption, not a claim about their inner constitution.

The epistemological level is not developed in this work. Its treatment requires multidisciplinary development beyond the present scope.

**Aristotelian correspondence** *(philosophical correspondence — interpretive layer; not a formal result of the GSF).* Aristotle's metaphysics articulates two of the three levels implicitly: the *ontological* level (the form and matter that constitute a substance — what a thing *is*) and the *teleological* level (the final cause — what a thing *tends toward*, the actualization of its potential). In Aristotle's terms, a UoC is always somewhere between *potentia* (dynamis, the configuration away from its structural attractor) and *actus* (energeia, the configuration at its attractor), oriented by its *telos* toward the latter.

What Aristotle's framework does not formalize — and what the UoC framework adds as a third, distinct layer — is the mechanism by which a system *participates in its own transformation*. In Aristotle, the movement from potentiality to actuality is driven by the telos, but the system's own self-knowledge and its capacity to modulate its own trajectory toward or away from its purpose have no formal representation. The epistemological modulation operator ($\Gamma_E$, §1.5.1) formalizes precisely this: a UoC's capacity to know itself and, from that self-knowledge, either accelerate its convergence toward its structural purpose or resist it (structural resistance, apparent attractors — developed in Ch9 §9.8).

The GSF is therefore not a reformulation of Aristotelian metaphysics but a formal extension of it: the ontological and teleological levels map directly onto Aristotle's categories, while the epistemological level — left formally open in this work — completes the structure with a layer that Aristotle did not supply. This opening is deliberate: the formal constitution of the epistemological level is an open question that the framework is designed to receive, not one it claims to close.

## 1.3 Intrinsic Variables

The ontological level is organized around four questions that any operative UoC must answer. These four are proposed as a minimal generative hypothesis: none can be removed without losing the conditions for a UoC to exist (§1.3.1); and — as argued formally in Ch3 via algebraic closure — no fifth variable adds expressive power within the chosen formal configuration space. The necessity argument is developed in §1.3.1; the sufficiency and completeness arguments are developed in Ch3.

The four questions are:

1. *What is my boundary?* → **S** (Singularity)
2. *What transformations can I generate?* → **A** (Agency)
3. *Where do I go by my own nature?* → **I** (Impulse)
4. *What must I exchange to persist?* → **R** (Relation)

*Note on register.* These questions are formulated at the universal structural level — valid for any UoC at any ρ, including physical and biological systems operating as units of coherence. At the ego ρ-level, where the epistemological level is positive, they instantiate as first-person questions: *Who am I? What choices can I make? What is my structural impulse? How do I relate?* The structural formulation is the general form; the first-person formulation is specific to units of consciousness with an active epistemological level.

Each question admits a different mathematical type of answer (scalar or vector); each type is the minimum structure required by its question. The derivation of these types is given in Ch2. The manifestation of each variable at different ρ levels of the recursive hierarchy is developed in Chapter 5; a representative cross-section is noted under each variable below.

The four intrinsic variables are:

**S — Singularity** (scalar)

*Question answered: What is my boundary?*

The degree to which the UoC distinguishes itself from its environment. S encodes the boundary between self and non-self — not necessarily a physical membrane, but any boundary (physical, energetic, informational) that separates the UoC's internal organization from its exterior. Without S, there is no unit — only an undifferentiated field.

*S = 0*: no boundary, no unit.
*S > 0*: operative boundary, UoC exists.

| ρ level | Boundary expression |
|---------|---------------------|
| Soul (ρ → 0) | Pure presence — "I AM" without predicate |
| Ego | Narrative identity — "I am X, Y, Z" |
| Biology | Multicellular organism — physical membrane, immune boundary |
| Cell | Cellular membrane — lipid bilayer |

**A — Agency** (vector)

*Question answered: What transformations can I generate?*

The capacity of the UoC to generate transformations — in its own configuration or in its environment. **A** has both magnitude (how much capacity) and direction (toward what). Without **A**, the UoC cannot respond or act; it is passive with respect to self-generated transformation.

| ρ level | Agency expression |
|---------|-------------------|
| Soul | Pure free will — unconditioned choice |
| Ego | Conditioned choice — within learned patterns |
| Biology | Instinctive regulation — homeostatic control |
| Cell | Adaptive response — gene expression modulation |

**I — Impulse** (vector)

*Question answered: Where do I go by my own nature?*

The intrinsic orientation of the UoC — where it tends by its own nature in the absence of external forces. **I** breaks the symmetry among all possible directions of action, making directed behavior possible. Without **I**, agency has no differential orientation; every action is intrinsically equivalent.

*Note*: Impulse (**I**) is distinct from intention, which is epistemological. Impulse is ontological — it operates prior to any cognitive or deliberate process. A living cell exhibits impulse (drives that arise from its chemistry) without any deliberate intention.

| ρ level | Impulse direction |
|---------|-------------------|
| Soul | Creative expression, inter-evolution |
| Ego | Reactive polarity — attraction and aversion |
| Biology | Homeostatic drive — equilibrium seeking |
| Cell | Survival drive — maintain gradients, avoid apoptosis |

**R — Relation** (vector)

*Question answered: What must I exchange to persist?*

The mode and direction of exchange between the UoC and its environment. An isolated system cannot maintain its organization indefinitely; relation is the structural mechanism by which a UoC maintains local organization. **R** is not a consequence of singularity but its condition of possibility: identity requires differentiation, and differentiation requires alterity.

| ρ level | Relation mode |
|---------|---------------|
| Soul | Inter-evolution — co-evolution with other UoCs |
| Ego | Security-seeking bonds — attachments for self-preservation |
| Biology | Symbiosis — metabolic and immunological exchange |
| Cell | Functional coordination — tissue coherence |

*Note on the Biology row.* "Biology" denotes multicellular biological systems as a ρ-level — the level at which the GSF maps to autopoietic organization (see §1.8.1 and Ch4 §4.3.1). At the scale of the personal hierarchy (Ch4), the biological UoC instantiates specifically as the body of the individual.

### 1.3.1 Necessity of the Four Variables

The absence of any of the four makes the UoC non-operative:

- Without S: no unit exists; nothing to have agency, impulse, or relation.
- Without A: the unit exists but cannot act; it is passive with respect to self-generated transformation.
- Without I: the unit has capacity but no orientation; all actions are structurally equivalent.
- Without R: the unit is isolated; it cannot maintain its organization indefinitely (thermodynamic openness — not derived here).

These are structural arguments for necessity, not formal proofs; the formal algebraic treatment is in Ch3.

**Remark 1.1 (Minimal Ontological Closure and Structural Determinacy).** The four-attribute structure {S, **A**, **I**, **R**} makes three substantive claims that go beyond the necessity argument above:

- **Minimality** *(Condition 1)*: none of the four is derivable from the other three within the G(3) algebraic structure. The formal argument is in Ch3.
- **Completeness** *(Condition 2 — conjecture)*: every intrinsic structural property of an operative UoC is expressible as a function of {S, **A**, **I**, **R**} under the correspondence rules of Ch1–Ch3. No irreducible fifth attribute exists within the G(3) framework.
- **Uniqueness** *(Condition 3)*: for any coherent entity to which the GSF applies, the mapping from that entity's observable properties to the four structural slots is *uniquely forced* by Conditions 1 and 2 — it is not a free parametric choice. No two inequivalent assignments of an entity's properties to {S, **A**, **I**, **R**} can simultaneously satisfy Conditions 1 and 2 while producing structurally identical predictions for that entity.

*Coherent entity*: any system for which the four attributes are simultaneously definable and mutually non-degenerate — one that has genuine internal organization, not a mere aggregate. Autopoietic systems (cells, organisms — Maturana and Varela 1980) are the paradigm case at biological levels; elementary particles and bound composite systems are the paradigm case at physical levels. A thermodynamic aggregate without internal self-measure (a gas in equilibrium, an inert rock) does not qualify, as no non-trivial S is definable independently of its relational properties.

The framework is falsified — or requires structural revision — under any of the following three conditions:

1. An irreducible UoC property is exhibited that cannot be mapped to any combination of {S, **A**, **I**, **R**} under the Ch1 correspondence rules. This would indicate **incompleteness**: the formal space does not capture the full ontological structure.
2. Two of the four attributes are shown to be derivable from each other within G(3). This would indicate **non-minimality**: the attribute set is redundant and the structural basis is smaller than claimed.
3. A coherent entity is exhibited for which two or more inequivalent assignments of its properties to {S, **A**, **I**, **R**} equally satisfy Conditions 1 and 2 while yielding structurally indistinguishable predictions. This would indicate **underdetermination**: the algebraic architecture does not uniquely resolve the ontological decomposition of that entity, and the framework is either being applied beyond its valid domain or requires a refinement of its correspondence rules at that level.

**A note on the count of four.** The number four is not an independent empirical hypothesis — it is grounded in the grade structure of G(3): grades 0 through 3 correspond to S, **A**, **I**, **R** respectively (developed in Ch2–Ch3). If a more parsimonious description were found (fewer irreducible attributes), the appropriate revision would be to examine whether G(3) is the right algebraic framework, not to declare the GSF falsified wholesale. Similarly, if a fifth attribute were required, the question would shift to whether a higher-dimensional algebra (e.g., G(4), with five grades) better captures the UoC's structure. The testable claim is the **correspondence between the G(3) grade structure and the irreducible ontological structure of UoCs** — a conjecture that Ch3 develops formally but does not close.

## 1.4 Inherent Variables

From the four intrinsic variables, two canonical derived observables are defined. These are not additional degrees of freedom — they are constitutively defined from the intrinsic variables and cannot be absent when the UoC is operative. They are called *inherent* variables. The choice of operations (scalar multiplication for Force, cross product for Field) is motivated by the grade structure of G(3) and derived in Ch2–Ch3; here they are introduced as definitions.

$$\mathbf{Force} = S\,\mathbf{A}$$

The expression of identity through capacity: $S \in \mathbb{R}$ scales the agency vector $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ (scalar multiplication, not dot product). Force has the direction of **A** and magnitude $|S||\mathbf{A}|$. It characterizes *how the UoC acts*: the will of the unit as it manifests through its capacity for transformation. Force is also the **cohesive force** of the UoC — the expression of identity through capacity that holds the unit together as a distinct operative entity and gives its existence structural meaning.

$\mathbf{Force} = \mathbf{0}$ when $S = 0$ (no unit) or $|\mathbf{A}| = 0$ (no capacity). Both are necessary: without Force, the unit's organization cannot sustain itself.

**Field** = **I** × **R**

The structural field vector generated by the tension between impulse and relation. Field has a direction perpendicular to both I and R, and a magnitude proportional to the sine of the angle between them:

$$|\text{Field}| = |\mathbf{I}||\mathbf{R}|\sin\theta$$

where θ is the angle between **I** and **R**.

Field characterizes *the structural atmosphere in which the UoC operates* — the relational-motivational tension from which the UoC orients itself. Its qualitative expression (what that field *feels like* at the epistemological level) varies with the configuration; the field itself is pre-cognitive and pre-phenomenal.

### 1.4.1 Operativity Condition

The structural field of a UoC is operative when **I** and **R** are not parallel:

> **Condition**: **I** ∦ **R** ↔ Field ≠ 0

When **I** ∥ **R** (θ = 0° or 180°): Field = 0. The UoC exists and has Force, but generates no structural field. This is a degenerate configuration: the UoC cannot orient itself toward its purpose, and the epistemological level — if non-null — cannot be activated. This condition is structurally analogous to a system with a source and a medium oriented in the same direction, producing no transverse field.

Maximum field is achieved when **I** ⊥ **R** (θ = 90°):

$$|\text{Field}|_{\max} = |\mathbf{I}||\mathbf{R}|$$

## 1.5 The State Vector and the Configuration Matrix

**The primary state vector.** The complete dynamical state of a UoC is encoded in:

$$\xi := (S,\, \mathbf{A},\, \mathbf{I},\, \mathbf{R}) \;\in\; \mathcal{V} = \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{R}^3$$

This is a 10-dimensional real vector. The dynamics of a UoC — its evolution over time — are formulated at this level: the evolution law governs $\dot{\xi}(t)$ (Ch9 §9.4).

The canonical derived observables are defined from $\xi$:

$$\mathbf{Force}(\xi) = S\,\mathbf{A}, \qquad \text{Field}(\xi) = \mathbf{I} \times \mathbf{R}$$

**Postulate 1.1 (Configuration Matrix).** For every operative UoC, there exists a matrix-valued functional

$$\Gamma = \Gamma(\xi) \;\in\; \mathrm{Sym}(4) \oplus \mathfrak{so}(4)$$

called the **configuration matrix**, encoding the pairwise coupling structure of the intrinsic variables. $\Gamma$ is not the state of the UoC — it is derived from the state $\xi$ and encodes the geometric relationships between the intrinsic variables at each moment. Its symmetric part $\Gamma_s$ governs dissipative (coordination) coupling; its antisymmetric part $\Gamma_a$ governs reactive (field-like) coupling. The full construction of $\Gamma(\xi)$ is developed in Ch5.

*Why two objects?* $\xi$ is the *primary state* (what the UoC is); $\Gamma(\xi)$ is the *coupling structure* (how the UoC's parts relate to each other). The health conditions of the UoC (structural operativity, full rank, positive-definite symmetric part) are conditions on $\Gamma(\xi)$, not on $\xi$ directly. The purpose function and the dynamics are most naturally expressed in terms of $\Gamma$, which is why $\Gamma$ — rather than $\xi$ raw — is the central object of the subsequent chapters.

The trajectory of a UoC through state space over time is $\xi(t)$, with induced configuration matrix $\Gamma(t) = \Gamma[\xi(t)]$. For a UoC with a non-null teleological level, the purpose acts as a structural attractor in $\mathcal{V}$: the system's dynamics tend toward a specific $\xi^* \in \mathcal{V}$ (existence postulated in Ch9, Postulate 9.1).

### 1.5.1 Modulation by the Epistemological Level

When the epistemological level is non-null, it acts as a modulation operator on $\Gamma(\xi)$. Denote the epistemological modulation as $\Gamma_E: \mathrm{Sym}(4) \oplus \mathfrak{so}(4) \to \mathrm{Sym}(4) \oplus \mathfrak{so}(4)$ — a bounded operator on the configuration matrix space (Ch5). The effective configuration matrix becomes:

$$\Gamma_{\text{eff}} = \Gamma_E\bigl(\Gamma(\xi)\bigr)$$

The internal mechanism of this modulation is not specified here — it is the subject of the epistemological level, outside the scope of this work. What is observable at the ontological level is its effect on the derived observables:

$$\mathbf{Force}_{\text{eff}} = S_{\text{eff}}\,\mathbf{A}_{\text{eff}}, \qquad \text{Field}_{\text{eff}} = \mathbf{I}_{\text{eff}} \times \mathbf{R}_{\text{eff}}$$

where $(S_\text{eff}, \mathbf{A}_\text{eff}, \mathbf{I}_\text{eff}, \mathbf{R}_\text{eff})$ are the effective intrinsic variable values under the modulation.

**Postulate 1.2 (Modulation bound).** The epistemological modulation does not alter the structural bounds of the intrinsic variables — it modulates their *effective expression* within those bounds. In particular:

- $|\mathbf{A}_\text{eff}| \leq |\mathbf{A}|$: the effective agency cannot exceed the structural agency.
- The directions of **A**_eff, **I**_eff, **R**_eff may differ from the unmodulated values.

This is a structural postulate, not a derived constraint. It reflects the interpretation that the epistemological level can redirect but not amplify the UoC's ontological capacity.

*Possible derivation*: The epistemological modulation may be formalizable as a rotation-and-scaling operator on the vectors **A**, **I**, **R** within the space they span. This connection is left for future development.

## 1.6 UoC as Nonlinear Dynamical System

A UoC with a non-null teleological level may be modeled as a nonlinear dynamical system that admits attractor dynamics. A UoC is distinguished from a generic nonlinear dynamical system by **recursive generativity**: each UoC produces emergent organization that constitutes the substrate for a UoC at the next level of the hierarchy. A dynamical system may have attractors without generating substrate for higher-level organization; that generation is what makes the UoC framework more than a reformulation of general systems theory.

Its state evolves as:

$$\frac{d\xi}{dt} = f\bigl(\xi,\, \Gamma_E,\, \rho\bigr)$$

where $f$ encodes the dynamics of the primary state under the influence of the purpose (via the purpose function $P(\Gamma(\xi))$ — defined in Ch1) and the epistemological modulation $\Gamma_E$. The purpose appears as a constraint on $f$ rather than as a separate argument: $f$ must satisfy $dP/dt \leq 0$ along autonomous trajectories (Ch9 §9.4). Smoothness conditions on $f$ (Lipschitz continuity, existence and uniqueness of trajectories) are stated in Ch9 §9.4.

The nature of the attractor depends on the configuration. The following table presents candidate phenomenological interpretations — not derived classifications — for selected ρ levels:

| Configuration | Attractor type (candidate) | Attractor content (candidate) |
|--------------|----------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Soul (ρ → 0) | Stable equilibrium (maximum coherence) | Wisdom — structural coherence fully realized |
| Ego | Dissipative (self-preservation) | Identity maintenance, security |
| Biology | Dissipative (homeostasis) | Physiological equilibrium |
| Cell | Dissipative (homeostasis) | Biochemical equilibrium, viability |

*Observation*: The transition between attractor types — corresponding to a fundamental reconfiguration of the UoC — is structurally analogous to a bifurcation in dynamical systems theory. This is a possible formal derivation for future work.

*Note on structural purpose.* The teleological level formalizes the structural attractor of $\xi(t)$ — a property arising from the UoC's own organization, intrinsic in the same sense as the intrinsic variables (§1.2). The attractor $\xi^*$ is postulated in Ch9 (Postulate 9.1); it is not guaranteed by the dynamics alone. Its content varies by ρ (homeostasis, self-preservation, structural coherence), but its structural existence is a constant feature of operative UoCs. The key distinction is between *orientation toward* the attractor — which is structural — and *reaching* it — which is dynamic. The epistemological level, when non-null, is the mechanism by which a UoC participates in its own convergence or divergence.

## 1.7 Scope and Open Questions

The following questions are acknowledged but outside the scope of this chapter:

- **Sufficiency of the four intrinsic variables**: the argument that no fifth variable is needed. Addressed in Ch3 via geometric algebra.
- **Derivation of the vector structure**: why S is scalar and **A**, **I**, **R** are vectors. Addressed in Chapter 2.
- **Construction of Γ(ξ)**: the explicit form of the configuration matrix as a functional of the state vector — defined in Ch5.
- **Properties of interacting UoCs**: fusion, reproduction, union, and other compositional behaviors. Defined formally in Ch7 using the algebraic objects of Ch5.
- **Epistemological level**: its variables, structure, and how Γ_E is formally constituted. Left for future work.
- **Cosmological context**: the question of why the universe produces UoCs, and whether the substrate-UoC recursion has a cosmological first term, is acknowledged as a motivating context but excluded from the formal development.

## 1.8 Relation to Existing Frameworks

### 1.8.1 Autopoiesis (Maturana and Varela)

The concept of autopoiesis — a system that produces and maintains its own organization through its own operations — is structurally adjacent to the UoC framework. A living cell satisfies the intrinsecity criterion (§1.2): its boundary is self-assembled, its regulatory capacity is emergent from its metabolic network, and its drives arise from its own chemistry. The UoC framework is structurally compatible with the autopoietic tradition of Maturana and Varela, while extending self-organizing principles beyond the biological domain into a recursive ontological hierarchy. Autopoiesis is constitutively biological; the UoC framework applies to levels below (physical) and above (psychological, cosmological) the biological, treating biology as one level of the ρ gradient rather than the defining case.

### 1.8.2 Process Philosophy (Whitehead)

Whitehead's process philosophy understands reality as constituted by events — *actual occasions* — rather than by substances. Each actual occasion is a discrete moment of becoming that prehends (takes into account) prior occasions and then perishes, contributing its content as objective data for subsequent occasions.

The UoC framework occupies a different but complementary ontological register. Where Whitehead's actual occasions describe *how reality occurs* — the temporal pulse of becoming — the UoC describes *what organizational structure sustains what occurs*. On this reading, the four intrinsic variables {S, **A**, **I**, **R**} constitute the organizational conditions that any actual occasion must instantiate to be the occasion of a UoC. *(Unit of consciousness context:* when the epistemological level is positive, this UoC is a unit of consciousness rather than a purely physical event — a unit of coherence operating without epistemological modulation.)

The two frameworks are not in competition; they address different aspects of the same reality — the process level and the structural level — and may prove formally integrable. A possible bridge is a map Γ_t → AO_t (actualization) together with a prehensive transition AO_t → Γ_{t+1} (inheritance), making Γ(t) the structural residue of a sequence of discrete actualizations. This formalization is reserved for future development (Appendix TBD).

### 1.8.3 Integrated Information Theory (Tononi)

Integrated Information Theory (IIT) proposes that consciousness corresponds to integrated information Φ — a measure of the degree to which a system generates information beyond its parts. IIT and the UoC framework address different questions: IIT addresses the measurement of informational integration as a correlate of consciousness; the UoC framework addresses the ontological structural organization that constitutes a UoC. *(Unit of consciousness context:* at the psychological and biological levels where the epistemological level is positive, a UoC is a unit of consciousness — the plausible site for an IIT-type analysis of its knowing processes.) The two frameworks are complementary but operate at different explanatory levels. A possible future connection: the epistemological level of a UoC (outside the scope of this work) is a plausible site for an IIT-type analysis of how information is integrated within the UoC's knowing processes.

### 1.8.4 The Hard Problem (Chalmers)

Chalmers's hard problem asks why physical processes give rise to phenomenal experience — why there is "something it is like" to be a conscious system. The UoC framework adopts methodological agnosticism with respect to the hard problem: it models the structural organization of units of consciousness without making a commitment regarding why phenomenal experience exists. This is not a dismissal of the hard problem but a recognition that structural organization and phenomenal experience are distinct explanatory targets. The framework's contribution is to the former; the latter remains open.
